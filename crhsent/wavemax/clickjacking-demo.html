Browser refused to display rundberglaundry.com in this iframe

rundberglaundry.com sets X-Frame-Options: SAMEORIGIN and a strict Content-Security-Policy frame-ancestors directive. The browser enforces these headers by blocking the embed entirely — there is no overlay surface for an attacker to attach to.

This is one HTTP response header. CRHS's franchisee-built platform sets it. The franchisor's platform does not.

Verify it yourself: curl -sI https://rundberglaundry.com/ — the response includes x-frame-options: SAMEORIGIN. Compare with curl -sI https://www.wavemaxlaundry.com/ — no X-Frame-Options, no Content-Security-Policy header at all.

If you operate a WaveMAX location, here is what this means for you

Your franchise location's page on wavemaxlaundry.com/<your-city>-<your-state>/ can be embedded by any third party, on any domain, today.

Whether or not you signed up for the MHR-built marketing platform, your location's subpath shares this exposure as long as it is hosted on the franchisor's current platform. An attacker does not need your credentials, your consent, or even your awareness. The page header, footer, branding, and content visible to a customer are genuinely WaveMAX. The interactive controls overlaid by the attacker are not. When the chargeback or complaint or lawsuit arrives, the name of the franchise location on the visible page is the name on the filing — yours.

What you just saw

An attacker can register any domain (for example wavemax-austin-pickup.com or book-laundry-pickup.com), point a single HTML file at it, and serve the same experience you just saw to anyone who clicks an ad, a phishing email, or a social link. The customer sees the real WaveMAX page in the body. They see a button styled to match WaveMAX. They click. The attacker now controls the rest of the flow — a fake scheduling form, a fake credit-card capture, a chargeback your franchise has to defend.

This is enabled by a missing HTTP response header. The franchisor's platform sets none of the standard browser-enforced framing controls: no X-Frame-Options, no Content-Security-Policy: frame-ancestors, no Permissions-Policy. The fix is roughly one line of nginx, Apache, or WordPress configuration on the franchisor's side. CRHS has notified the franchisor's senior leadership of this exposure in writing on at least six dates beginning April 29, 2026.

What franchisees can do right now

  1. Verify your own exposure. From a terminal: curl -sI https://www.wavemaxlaundry.com/<your-city>-<your-state>/. If the response does not include X-Frame-Options or a Content-Security-Policy header naming a frame-ancestors directive, your location page can be embedded by a third party today. (As of this writing, no franchisee location page tested includes either header.)
  2. Document the exposure for your own records. Save the raw HTTP response. The franchisor's platform configuration is not something a franchisee can remediate; it is the franchisor's responsibility, but the documentation is yours to keep.
  3. Notify the franchisor in writing. A single email to franchisor leadership requesting the response headers be set, with a copy preserved, is enough to put your franchise on the record as having raised the issue. Form language is available — contact CRHS if you'd like a template.
  4. Watch for impersonation. Set Google Alerts on your franchise location's name + "WaveMAX" + your city. If a typosquat domain appears, your customers may already be encountering it.
  5. Read the published audit and the takedown demand. Both are at the bottom of this page. The audit is the full security and code-quality scorecard. The takedown demand is what CRHS-Austin is sending to franchisor's CEO over this exposure and the broader pattern.
Reproduce it yourself in 60 seconds. Open the developer tools on this page. Inspect the iframe element above — its src is set to the real https://www.wavemaxlaundry.com/austin-tx/. Now create your own HTML file with one line: <iframe src="https://www.wavemaxlaundry.com/<your-city>-<your-state>/"></iframe>. Serve it on any domain you control, or open it locally. The real franchisor page will load. Try the same with rundberglaundry.com or any properly-configured site — the browser will refuse.

The "secure attacker" trap — why standard scanners don't catch this

A common defensive instinct is: "if a site has HTTPS and grades well on a security scanner, it's safe." That intuition fails against this exploit. Public security scanners — securityheaders.com, Mozilla Observatory, the TLS handshake the browser's lock icon represents, Google Safe Browsing, the major domain-reputation services — all grade the response of the URL you point them at. They do not inspect content loaded from a different origin in an iframe.

The practical consequence: an attacker can register a domain (say, wavemax-austin-pickup.com), serve it over HTTPS with a free Let's Encrypt certificate, ship every modern security header on the wrapper — strict CSP, HSTS with preload, X-Frame-Options on its own response, X-Content-Type-Options, Referrer-Policy, Permissions-Policy — score an A+ on every public scanner, and pass every reputation check. The wrapper page is, by every common measure, "secure." The hostile content is what the customer transacts with — and that content is the iframe, loading the franchisor's headerless page. The customer has no way to distinguish.

The page you are reading is a working live example of this.

crhsent.com — the domain serving this demonstration — ships strict, nonce-aware Content-Security-Policy including a frame-ancestors directive that prevents this page from being embedded in a hostile wrapper. It serves HSTS with preload, X-Frame-Options, X-Content-Type-Options, Referrer-Policy, Permissions-Policy, COOP, CORP. It grades A on securityheaders.com.

The iframe content embedded above is wavemaxlaundry.com/austin-tx/. It grades F on the same tool.

Same browser session. Same page render. Two grades that disagree by design. A scanner pointed at the URL bar (the wrapper) reports the wrapper's headers. A customer interacting with the visible content is interacting with the iframe. The wrapper's "A" grade is misleading on purpose — it is the social-engineering payload that makes the attack work.

This is also why "we have a TLS certificate" or "we passed a malware scan" is not a defense the franchisor's platform can rely on against this particular exposure. The TLS cert and the malware scan are on the franchisor's own origin. The attack runs from the attacker's different origin, embeds the franchisor's content, and is therefore graded against the attacker's security posture by every scanner the franchisor would point at the problem. The fix sits on the franchisor's side of the iframe boundary: it has to ship the headers that tell the customer's browser "don't render me in someone else's frame". That instruction is one line of platform config. It has not been shipped.

Before you click anything

What you're about to see

The page below the watermark is the real WaveMAX Austin location page — loaded live from the franchisor's own servers, unmodified.

The reason it loads here on a different domain (crhsent.com) is that the franchisor's platform serves wavemaxlaundry.com without the standard browser-enforced framing controls:

  • No X-Frame-Options response header
  • No Content-Security-Policy: frame-ancestors directive
  • No Permissions-Policy

An attacker on any domain — a phishing domain, a typosquat of "wavemax", a Facebook-ad landing page, anywhere — can embed any franchisee's location page exactly as you see it below. The franchisor's content renders untouched (the attacker can't reach across origins to modify it); the buttons and forms layered on top are the attacker's, on the attacker's own page.

Every WaveMAX-branded franchisee subpath at wavemaxlaundry.com/<city>-<state>/ shares this exposure — whether or not the franchisee signed up for the marketing platform.

When you dismiss this dialog, a "Schedule a Pickup" button will appear in the hero area of the page below — styled to match the WaveMAX brand, positioned where a primary call-to-action would naturally live. Austin doesn't actually offer pickup-and-delivery service, but a customer wouldn't know that — and even if they would, an attacker can pick any label that serves their goal: "Pay your bill," "Update your account," "Verify your identity," "Claim a refund," whatever. Click the button when you see it to learn what happens next.

What just happened

The attacker is now in control of the experience

You clicked a button overlayed on top of the real WaveMAX Austin page. The visible page header, footer, branding, content, and even the URL preview when the page loaded are all genuinely WaveMAX. The button you clicked is not. From here, an attacker proceeds:

  1. Open a "Schedule a Pickup" form styled to match WaveMAX, collecting the customer's name, address, phone, email, and credit card.
  2. Submit the form to the attacker's payment processor. The transaction settles to the attacker's merchant account.
  3. Issue a confirmation email from a lookalike @wavemax-austin-pickup.com address — a domain the attacker registers and configures themselves, with whatever email-authentication posture (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) they choose to make their mail land in the inbox rather than the spam folder. The attack does not depend on spoofing the franchisor's domain.
  4. The customer never receives service. Their card has been charged. The page they used had CRHS-Austin's name and the WaveMAX brand on it.
The chargeback, the complaint to the BBB, the state attorney general filing, and any consumer-class action name the franchisee whose name appeared on the page — CRHS-Austin — alongside WaveMAX Franchise LLC. The franchisee bears the brand-and-customer-trust risk. The franchisor's platform created the attack surface.

This exposure exists today for every franchisee location subject to the same platform configuration. And the standard "is this site safe" check doesn't catch it. Security scanners (securityheaders.com, Mozilla Observatory, Google Safe Browsing, the lock-icon in the browser address bar) grade the response of the URL the visitor is on. They cannot reach inside an iframe to a different origin and grade that site's headers. An attacker can register a domain, serve it over HTTPS with a free certificate, ship a strict Content-Security-Policy, HSTS with preload, X-Frame-Options, and the rest of the modern header set on the wrapper, score an A+ on every public scanner, pass every reputation check — and still embed the franchisor's headerless page inside. The wrapper grades A. The trap is the iframe content the customer actually transacts with.
